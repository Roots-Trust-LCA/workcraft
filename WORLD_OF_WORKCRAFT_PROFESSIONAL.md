# World of Workcraft — Professional Coordination Framework

**A distributed operating system for cooperative organizational networks.**

*co-op.us extended: from single hubs to peer-to-peer federated networks, with explicit specialization, communication, and data ownership.*

---

## Part I: The Shift from Hierarchy to Federation

### From Tree to Federation

In the co-op.us genesis design, each hub operates as an independent **node** — a single organizational structure rooted in place, governed through the seven-dimensional lens (e/H-LAM/T/S).

World of Workcraft extends this: **together, the nodes form a federation.**

A federation is not a collection of organizations managed by a central authority. It is an **ecosystem** where:
- Each node is autonomous and rooted in its place
- Nodes are connected through peer-to-peer relationships, not top-down authority
- Resources (capital, information, capability) flow through the network
- Communication is local first, global when needed
- Data is held cooperatively, not centrally

**The Federation Principles** establish what it means to be a node in the larger ecosystem:

1. **Node Autonomy**: Each hub governs itself completely. No central authority can override local decisions.
2. **Portable Identity**: Your professional profile (role, expertise, standing) is recognized across all nodes.
3. **Shared Primitives**: All nodes use $CLOUD, the chain, and the patronage engine. These are non-negotiable.
4. **Async-First Coordination**: Communication defaults to asynchronous and local. Synchronous coordination only when needed.
5. **Data Sovereignty**: Each node holds its own data. The federation has no central database.

### The Seven Layers at Node Scale

Each hub is structured through seven layers (the e/H-LAM/T/S stack extended):

| Layer | What It Holds | Scope |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| **e/ Ecology** | Watershed, geology, season, climate. The physical place. | Local |
| **H/ Human** | Members, contributors, stewards. The people. | Hub + portable to federation |
| **L/ Language** | Shared vocabulary, naming conventions, protocols. | Hub-first, federated |
| **A/ Artifacts** | Projects, documents, tools, ventures. The durable work. | Hub + shared across nodes |
| **M/ Methodology** | Governance, coordination patterns, decision protocols. | Hub-specific |
| **T/ Training** | Learning, capability building, knowledge commons. | Hub + shared resources |
| **S/ Sessions** | Gatherings, conversations, convergence events. | Hub + cross-hub sessions |

At the **hub level**, these seven layers are deeply local — parameterized for the specific place and people.

At the **federation level**, these seven layers become **communication channels and coordination surfaces** where nodes can interact without losing autonomy.

## Part II: Professional Development — Domain Expertise

### From Professional Profile to Domain Expertise

In the base design, a member selects two crafts (primary + secondary) and establishes one of 64 professional profiles. This is the starting point.

World of Workcraft adds **depth**: professional profiles evolve into **domain expertise** through sustained practice.

### The Expertise Development System

**Requirements for Domain Expertise:**
- Minimum 20 contributions in the professional profile
- At least 40 days in the community
- Demonstrated mastery (reviewed by practice community peers)
- Active participation in at least one venture or committee

When these requirements are met, the professional profile **branches** into one of six possible domain expertise areas, each representing a different *way* of practicing that craft.

### Example: Terraformer (Code × Earth) Domain Expertise Paths

The **Terraformer** (Code × Earth) can develop expertise in one of:

| Domain Expertise | Focus | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Regenerative Systems** | Design for regeneration. Building systems that heal. | Ecological monitoring tools, regenerative ag data systems, carbon accounting |
| **Infrastructure Architecture** | Shape the landscape. Large-scale infrastructure. | Watershed mapping, ecosystem modeling, bioregional networks |
| **Network Systems** | Underground connections. Hidden networks and mycelial thinking. | Peer-to-peer infrastructure, graph databases, knowledge graphs, mesh networks |
| **Living Systems Integration** | Code that works with biology, not against it. | Pollination algorithms, swarm coordination, biological integration |
| **Systems Recovery** | Repair and recovery. Fixing what was broken. | Legacy system refactoring, ecological restoration tech, data recovery |
| **Performance Engineering** | Raw capability. Precision, scale, optimization. | High-performance systems, critical infrastructure, industrial-grade tools |

No domain expertise path is "better" — only different *expressions* of the craft. A professional can redirect their expertise development once per season (every ~3 months).

### Domain Expertise Across All Professional Profiles

Each of the 64 professional profiles branches into 6 domain expertise paths, creating **384 possible expertise combinations** in the system.

The naming is always clear: the professional profile name + expertise direction.

**Examples across crafts:**

- **Poet** (Word × Word) can develop expertise in: Narrative Architecture, Documentation Systems, Translation, Content Strategy, Critical Analysis, or Knowledge Preservation
- **Navigator** (Water × Code) can develop expertise in: Systems Integration, Community Architecture, Mediation Design, Information Architecture, Bridge Building, or Stakeholder Relations
- **Catalyst** (Fire × Fire) can develop expertise in: Organizational Transformation, Craft Excellence, Change Management, Conflict Resolution, Strategic Protection, or Impact Amplification

### The Expertise Development Path

```
Professional Profile (Primary × Secondary)
  ├─ Domain Expertise Path A
  ├─ Domain Expertise Path B
  ├─ Domain Expertise Path C
  ├─ Domain Expertise Path D
  ├─ Domain Expertise Path E
  └─ Domain Expertise Path F
```

A professional's **contribution history** includes all their expertise development — past and present. This creates a visible **career journey**.

## Part III: The Federation Ecosystem — Peer-to-Peer Communication

### The Problem: Nodes in Isolation

A single hub can flourish. But hubs in isolation face limits:
- Knowledge doesn't flow between communities
- Ventures that should be cross-hub remain siloed
- There's no platform for ecosystem-level coordination
- Data lives in separate systems, creating friction

### The Solution: Federation Communication Layers

World of Workcraft defines four communication surfaces, each operating at different scope and rhythm:

#### 1. **Local Channels** (Within a Hub)

Each hub has a **communication tree** — a set of channels organized by the seven layers:

```
Hub: Techne (Boulder, Colorado)

├─ e/ Ecology
│  ├─ #watershed-updates
│  ├─ #seasonal-planning
│  └─ #local-projects
│
├─ H/ Human
│  ├─ #introductions
│  ├─ #practice-community-gatherings (by primary craft)
│  └─ #member-announcements
│
├─ L/ Language
│  ├─ #terminology
│  ├─ #naming-debates
│  └─ #translation
│
├─ A/ Artifacts
│  ├─ #project-updates (by venture)
│  ├─ #resource-library
│  └─ #tools-and-templates
│
├─ M/ Methodology
│  ├─ #governance
│  ├─ #meeting-notes
│  └─ #decision-tracking
│
├─ T/ Training
│  ├─ #learning-circles
│  ├─ #skill-shares
│  └─ #knowledge-base
│
└─ S/ Sessions
   ├─ #upcoming-gatherings
   ├─ #convergence-prep
   └─ #event-notes
```

**Properties:**
- Fully local. No visibility to other nodes unless explicitly shared.
- Asynchronous-first. Tools for threaded discussion, not real-time chat.
- Moderated by hub stewards who know the context.
- Archive on local chain (immutable, verifiable).

---

#### 2. **Inter-Node Bridges** (Between Specific Hubs)

When two hubs want to coordinate, they form a **bridge** — a dedicated communication channel between them.

```
Bridge: Techne ←→ Nairobi Hub

├─ #shared-ventures
│  ├─ Solar Cooperative (technical coordination)
│  └─ Patronage Research (cross-hub learning)
│
├─ #knowledge-exchange
│  ├─ Ecological patterns in Africa vs. Americas
│  └─ Governance adaptations
│
└─ #economic-flows
   ├─ Cross-hub $CLOUD transactions
   └─ Shared royalty tracking
```

**Forming a Bridge:**
1. Hub A nominates a **bridge steward** — someone who deeply understands both communities
2. Hub B accepts or proposes someone different
3. Together they publish a **covenant** describing what the bridge is for and how decisions flow back to each hub
4. The bridge creates specific channels for that coordination
5. Either hub can dissolve the bridge at any time

**Properties:**
- Requires explicit consent from both hubs
- Decisions made on the bridge don't override local governance
- Bridge stewards are ambassadors, not authorities
- All activity on the bridge is mirrored to both hubs' local archives

---

#### 3. **Practice Forums** (Global Professional Communities)

The eight primary crafts define the eight **Practice Forums** — global coordination spaces for people practicing the same craft across all hubs.

```
Practice Forum: Code Practitioners (across all hubs)

├─ #infrastructure-standards
│  │ (coordinating data schema, chain versioning, $CLOUD mechanics)
│
├─ #craft-innovation
│  │ (sharing new patterns, libraries, tools)
│
├─ #expertise-mentoring
│  │ (experienced practitioners advising newcomers)
│
├─ #ecosystem-security
│  │ (coordinating on vulnerabilities, incident response)
│
└─ #forum-convergence
   (annual gathering of all Code practitioners)
```

**How Practice Forums Work:**
- Any member of the craft can read and participate
- Decisions are made by **consensus-seeking**, not voting
- Each forum publishes its own **best practices** and **patterns**
- A hub can choose to adopt or adapt forum standards
- If a hub rejects a critical standard (e.g., security), the federation can enforce via economic pressure (e.g., lowering trust score)

**Forum Officers** (one per hub, elected by local forum members):
- Bridge between local practice and global coordination
- Represent their hub's needs in forum conversations
- No executive power — guidance and consensus-building only

---

#### 4. **Federation Council** (Ecosystem-Level Coordination)

The **Federation Council** is where hubs coordinate on infrastructure, economics, and governance that affect the whole ecosystem.

```
Federation Council

├─ Council Members
│  └─ One steward per hub (rotated annually)
│
├─ Standing Committees
│  ├─ Patronage & Allocation (how $CLOUD distribution works across hubs)
│  ├─ Chain & Cryptography (protocol integrity)
│  ├─ Federation Economics (revenue sharing, infrastructure costs)
│  ├─ Ecology & Bioregionalism (coordinating e/ layer across places)
│  ├─ Learning & Knowledge Commons (T/ layer federation)
│  └─ Conflict Resolution (resolving hub disputes)
│
└─ Decision Protocols
   ├─ Consensus: All affected hubs must agree
   ├─ Substantial Consensus: 80%+ hubs agree; dissenters can opt-out
   ├─ Simple Majority: 50%+ hubs agree; all must implement
   └─ One Hub, Veto: Any hub can veto decisions that threaten its autonomy
```

**What the Federation Council Coordinates:**
- $CLOUD mechanics and inflation/deflation
- Chain schema and cryptographic standards
- Federation infrastructure (shared servers, CDNs, archival)
- Major vulnerabilities and security responses
- Admission of new hubs
- Dissolution of hubs that violate Federation Principles

**What the Federation Council Cannot Do:**
- Override a hub's local governance
- Require specific ventures or projects
- Set patronage formulas (those are local)
- Remove a member from the federation
- Dictate member expulsion from any hub

## Part IV: Engagement Types — Work Classification

### The Engagement Framework

Work is categorized by scope and coordination requirements:

| Engagement Type | Description | Professional Context |
|-----------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Standing Commitment** | Routine, recurring work | Operations, maintenance, heartbeat tasks |
| **Process Improvement** | Non-critical enhancements | Refactoring, documentation, tooling |
| **Strategic Initiative** | Core roadmap objectives | Product development, key deliverables |
| **Cross-Functional Initiative** | Multi-party coordinated work | Complex projects requiring role coordination |

### Engagement Structure

Each engagement includes:

1. **Scope Definition** — Clear objectives and deliverables
2. **Capacity Requirement** — Resource demand (time, compute, attention)
3. **Role Definitions** — Required functions and responsibilities
4. **Timeline** — Start, milestones, completion
5. **Allocation Framework** — How value is distributed upon completion

### Example: Cross-Functional Initiative Structure

```
Initiative: Pollination Sensor Network

Scope: Deploy monitoring infrastructure across three watersheds

Capacity Required:
  - Infrastructure Lead: 40 hours (Tank role)
  - Systems Developer: 60 hours (DPS role)
  - Field Researcher: 30 hours (Healer role)

Timeline: 6 weeks
  - Week 1-2: Infrastructure setup
  - Week 3-4: Sensor deployment
  - Week 5-6: Data validation

Allocation:
  - Total: 200 $CLOUD + royalty share
  - Distribution: Based on contribution verification
  - Bonus: Early completion, quality metrics
```

## Part V: Capacity Management — Resource Budgeting

### The Capacity Budget System

**Capacity Budget** tracks available resources (time, compute, attention) for each professional.

**Key Concepts:**

1. **Budget Allocation** — Each professional receives a capacity budget per cycle (daily/weekly)
2. **Capacity Cost** — Each engagement has a visible capacity requirement
3. **Utilization Tracking** — Real-time view of allocated vs. available capacity
4. **Recovery Period** — Renewal bonus after rest periods (prevents burnout)

### Capacity Dashboard

Each professional sees:

```
Professional: Maya
Role: Terraformer (Code × Earth)
Expertise: Living Systems Integration

Capacity Budget (Weekly):
  - Total: 100 units
  - Allocated: 65 units
  - Available: 35 units
  - Recovery Bonus: +10 units (after weekend rest)

Current Engagements:
  - Pollination Network (Cross-Functional): 40 units
  - Documentation Update (Process Improvement): 25 units

Upcoming:
  - Guild Standards Review (Standing Commitment): 15 units
```

### Why Capacity Visibility Matters

**Before:** Professionals commit to work without seeing total load → burnout, missed deadlines

**After:** Capacity requirements are visible before commitment → sustainable pacing, realistic planning

## Part VI: The Digital Infrastructure Trust

### The Problem: Centralization Through Defaults

When hubs rely on a single cloud provider (AWS, Google, etc.), they are not truly autonomous. The provider controls availability, pricing, data access, and can be forced to censure.

World of Workcraft solves this through the **Digital Infrastructure Trust** — a cooperative service that holds and operates computing infrastructure for the federation.

### The Trust Model

**Who Owns It:** Members of the Digital Infrastructure Trust are:
- Any member of any hub (in the federation) can join
- The trust is governed as a cooperative: one member, one vote
- Initial board: technical stewards from 3+ hubs (elected annually)

**What It Does:**
- Operates shared computing infrastructure (servers, storage, networking)
- Provides services to hubs on a cost-recovery basis
- Maintains data residency and privacy guarantees
- Handles archival and disaster recovery
- Coordinates security updates and incident response

**Services Offered:**

| Service | Cost | SLA | Notes |
|---------|------|-----|-------|
| **Chain Archive** | $CLOUD/byte/year | 99.9% uptime | Immutable storage of local chain + federation chain |
| **Message Storage** | $CLOUD/message/year | 99.9% uptime | Hub message archives, practice forums, bridges |
| **Compute** | $CLOUD/core-hour | Service-level agreement per use case | For patronage calculations, data processing, analysis |
| **Transfer** | $CLOUD/GB | Cost-based | Data movement between hubs, backups, replication |
| **Identity Service** | $CLOUD/identity/year | 99.99% uptime | Professional profile resolution, public key infrastructure, standing data |

**Example:** A hub storing 500GB of chain data, 50GB of messages, running quarterly patronage reports:
```
Chain Archive:       500 GB × $0.001/GB-year = $500/year ≈ 500 $CLOUD
Message Storage:     50 GB × $0.001/GB-year = $50/year ≈ 50 $CLOUD
Compute (quarterly): 4 runs × 2 hours × $0.05/core-hour ≈ 100 $CLOUD
Annual Cost:                                    ≈ 650 $CLOUD (~$65)
```

### Data Governance in the Trust

**Core Principles:**

1. **Data Locality**: Each hub's data is held in the region it operates in (Boulder data stays in Colorado, Nairobi data in Kenya). Exception: encrypted backups for disaster recovery.

2. **Encryption by Default**: Data at rest and in transit uses end-to-end encryption. Hubs control their own keys. The Trust never holds plaintext of sensitive data.

3. **Access Transparency**: Every access to data (even for backups or monitoring) is logged on the chain. Hubs can audit the Trust.

4. **Economic Efficiency**: The Trust operates at cost. Surplus revenue is returned to members as $CLOUD credits or reinvested in infrastructure.

5. **Exit Right**: Any hub can leave and retrieve its data in an open format (JSON, standard SQL dumps, etc.) with 60 days notice.

### Trust Operations

**Governance:**
- Annual election of board (3-5 stewards from different hubs)
- Quarterly financial reports (audited, posted on chain)
- Any member can propose infrastructure improvements
- Decisions made by consensus of board; major decisions by member vote

**Technical Oversight:**
- Independent security audits (annual, paid by trust)
- Open-source core infrastructure (code published, auditable)
- Bug bounty program (funded from operational budget)
- Incident response plan published and tested annually

**Exit & Failure Scenarios:**
- If the Trust dissolves: all data reverts to hubs in encrypted form
- If a hub leaves: data is exported within 60 days
- If infrastructure fails: geographically distributed backups ensure recovery within hours

## Part VII: Professional Standing — Recognition Levels

### The Standing System

Professional standing reflects community acknowledgment of sustained contribution:

| Standing | Requirements | Recognition |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Contributor** | 5+ verified contributions | Basic standing, full participation rights |
| **Steward** | 20+ contributions + domain expertise | Committee eligibility, mentorship role |
| **Principal** | 50+ contributions + demonstrated leadership | Governance weight, strategic input |

### Standing Progression

Standing advances through:

1. **Verified Contributions** — Work attested by peers or systems
2. **Domain Expertise Development** — Demonstrated mastery in focus area
3. **Community Investment** — Mentoring, standards development, governance participation
4. **Sustained Engagement** — Consistent participation over time

### Why Standing Matters

**Before:** All members have equal voice regardless of contribution → free rider problem, expertise undervalued

**After:** Standing reflects demonstrated commitment → governance weight aligns with investment, expertise is recognized

## Part VIII: Economic Flows — Patronage & Allocation

### The Patronage Engine

**Patronage** is the economic heart of the federation — how value flows to contributors.

**Allocation Formula (proposed):**
- 40% Labor — Verified contributions (time, expertise)
- 30% Revenue — Value generated by ventures
- 20% Capital — Financial investment in infrastructure
- 10% Community — Governance participation, mentorship

### Economic Transparency

Every professional sees:

```
Professional: Maya
Standing: Steward

Contribution Record (Current Cycle):
  - Cross-Functional Initiatives: 3 (120 hours verified)
  - Process Improvements: 5 (40 hours verified)
  - Standing Commitments: 8 (60 hours verified)
  - Total Verified: 220 hours

Allocation Projection:
  - Labor Share: 40% × (220 / total federation hours)
  - Standing Bonus: +5% (Steward level)
  - Expected Distribution: 850 $CLOUD

Patronage History:
  - Previous Cycle: 720 $CLOUD
  - Year-to-Date: 4,200 $CLOUD
```

### The Contribution Record

All work is recorded on the immutable chain:
- **What** — Engagement type, scope, deliverables
- **Who** — Contributors and roles
- **When** — Timestamp, cycle reference
- **Verification** — Attestations from peers or systems
- **Allocation** — Distribution upon completion

## Part IX: Why This Matters

### The Core Problem

Most organizational coordination faces a hard choice:
- **Centralize** for efficiency and coordination
- **Decentralize** for autonomy and resilience

World of Workcraft proposes a third way: **distributed coordination through explicit design**.

By making communication structures, professional development, and economic flows **legible and peer-to-peer**, we enable ecosystems that are:

- **Locally Rooted** — Each hub thrives in its specific place
- **Globally Coordinated** — Hubs see each other and can collaborate
- **Economically Just** — Value flows based on contribution, not concentration
- **Resilient** — No single point of failure; exit is always an option
- **Emergent** — New forms of coordination arise from practice, not planning

### Professional Benefits

**For Individuals:**
- Visible capability development
- Portable professional identity
- Transparent economic participation
- Sustainable capacity management

**For Organizations:**
- Clear engagement classification
- Cross-functional coordination without hierarchy
- Retention through recognition and growth paths
- Economic alignment with contribution

**For the Federation:**
- Resilient infrastructure (no central point of failure)
- Knowledge flow without extraction
- Economic justice built into the operating system
- Emergent coordination (standards arise from practice)
